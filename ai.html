How Far You've Come

On two communities and the practice of becoming.

The first time I had to read out loud in front of a class, the letters refused to hold still. I was seven, recently diagnosed with dyslexia, and I learned very quickly that the world tends to measure children by what they can do today — not by what they are slowly, painstakingly, becoming. The same year, my parents took me to an ice rink. It was, for a while, a place where words couldn't follow me.

For the next twelve years, hockey was the place I could be fluent. It carried me from Vancouver to San Jose at fourteen, to Hotchkiss in Connecticut at sixteen, and to the dressing room where, eventually, a labrum tear at nineteen made it clear that the sport that had given me a refuge could not be the one that defined me. The funny thing about losing a thing you've built your identity around is that the loss does not leave you empty. It leaves you ready.

That readiness came in the form of a rowing shell. I picked up an oar for the first time at Hotchkiss, almost by accident, and discovered something I had never had in hockey: a sport that rewards the exact part of me that dyslexia had spent a decade strengthening. Rowing isn't a sport of natural fluency. It's a sport of micro-corrections, of repetition, of small adjustments compounded across thousands of strokes. It is, in other words, how I had already learned to read.

I rowed at Loyola Marymount for three years, with a brief detour to Trinity College in Ireland, until the program was cut and I was, once again, in the position of having to start over. I transferred to Northeastern in 2023 — a school I hadn't originally considered — and somewhere between earning the Red and Black's Most Dedicated Athlete award my first year and being named captain the next, I started to understand that the through-line of my life is not hockey, or rowing, or even computer science. It is the practice of becoming.

Ability isn't what you achieve. It's how far you've come from where you started.

There are two communities that taught me that — the learning disability community and the rowing community — and they don't usually intersect. Most kids with dyslexia don't grow up to row at the Division I level. Most rowers don't grow up sounding out the same paragraph four times before they understand it. But the lessons turn out to be the same: a person is not the sum of what they can do today; they're the slope of what they're becoming. That belief is the reason LearningConn exists, the reason I'm building a real-time feedback system to make high-level rowing coaching more accessible, and the reason I want to spend my career returning what I've been given back to both of these communities.

If you've found this page, you've found the part of the site where I get to say all of that out loud. The rest of the site shows what I've built. This page is about why.

People should not be measured by their accomplishments but rather by how far they have come from where they started.
